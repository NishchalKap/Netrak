## Executive Summary

India's digital public safety problem has stopped being a "crime reporting" problem and become an **industrial-scale, cross-border fraud economy problem**. In 2025, Indians lost an estimated **₹22,495 crore (~US$2.7B)** to cyber fraud, with complaint volume up 24% to ~2.81 million cases, even as recovered/frozen value rose (1.2M SIMs deactivated, 1.33M mule accounts frozen, ₹5,489 crore recovered).<cite index="1-1">In 2025, authorities deactivated 1.2 million SIM cards, froze 1.33 million mule accounts, and recovered ₹5,489 crore.</cite> Digital arrest scams alone crossed **₹3,000 crore** in losses and became the subject of an unprecedented Supreme Court suo motu intervention that handed the CBI a pan-India mandate in December 2025. Counterfeit currency, while numerically smaller, remains structurally important: RBI's FY2025-26 Annual Report shows **97.6% of FICN is caught by commercial banks, not the RBI itself**, and ₹500 fake notes rose 20.5% year-on-year — evidence that detection has migrated almost entirely to the last mile of the banking system, where tooling is weakest.

The government response over the last 12 months has been unusually dense: RBI's MuleHunter.AI, NPCI's AI risk-scoring, DoT's Sanchar Saathi/Chakshu and CNAP caller-ID rollout, CERT-In's May 2026 AI-threat blueprint, I4C's ASTR/CyMAC/Pratibimb toolkit, and the incorporation of a dedicated **Indian Digital Payment Intelligence Corporation (IDPIC)** in October 2025 — a section-8 company mandated to build real-time, AI/ML-based fraud detection across the payments ecosystem. Yet nearly every one of these initiatives is described in its own official documentation as partial: MuleHunter.AI covers ~23 of India's ~150+ scheduled banks; RBI itself admits it cannot report mule-account outcomes because of fiduciary/RTI exemptions; CERT-In's blueprint is advisory, not binding; and NCRB does not track AI-driven cybercrime as a distinct category at all.

The commercial landscape splits into three tiers: (1) mature Indian KYC/identity RegTech (Signzy, HyperVerge, IDfy, Karza/Perfios) that has bolted on deepfake/liveness detection to existing onboarding rails; (2) global behavioral fraud platforms (Sardine, Unit21, Bureau ID) with no India-specific regulatory tuning; and (3) a counterfeit-currency detection layer that is **still dominated by 2005-era UV/IR/magnetic-ink hardware** — the AI-based mobile/computer-vision layer described in RFP-style challenge documents exists only as academic prototypes (IIT Kharagpur app, GitHub notebooks) and has not been commercialized at scale. This gap — a real-time, high-accuracy, phone-camera-based counterfeit detector usable by a bank teller or citizen — is the single clearest whitespace identified in this research.

# PART 1 — Government Initiatives

## 1.1 Reserve Bank of India (RBI)

**Purpose:** Protect the integrity of digital payments and physical currency; shift bank-side fraud detection from static rules to AI/ML.

**Key initiatives (last 12 months):**

- **MuleHunter.AI** — an AI/ML model built by the Reserve Bank Innovation Hub (RBIH), studying **19 behavioural signatures** of mule accounts across pooled bank/PSO data.<cite index="19-1">As of December 10, 2025, 23 banks have implemented the Reserve Bank of India's (RBI) MuleHunter.AI initiative, according to an RTI response that MediaNama received from the central bank.</cite> Pilot banks include Canara Bank, PNB, Bank of India, Bank of Baroda and AU Small Finance Bank.<cite index="19-1">Earlier in August 2025, the RBI's Chief General Manager (CGM) Suvendu Pati said that at least 15 banks had already implemented MuleHunter.AI.</cite> RBIH separately advertises **85%+ accuracy** for the model.<cite index="18-1">AI-powered fraud detection with 85%+ accuracy, and constant learning</cite>
 - *Limitation:* RBI **cannot disclose** mule-account outcome data, citing fiduciary confidentiality under the RTI Act — meaning independent verification of real-world efficacy is not currently possible.<cite index="19-1">the RBI said it cannot provide data on the number of mule accounts identified or acted upon through MuleHunter.AI... because the RBI holds that information in a fiduciary capacity with banks</cite> Coverage is also uneven: only 23 of India's ~150 scheduled commercial banks/cooperative banks had onboarded as of Dec 2025.
 - Technology: AI/ML transaction-and-account-behaviour pattern analysis, infrastructure-level shared model rather than per-bank build.<cite index="20-1">MuleHunter.AI is an infrastructure that uses databases from all banks and payment system operators.</cite>
 - Official link: https://rbihub.in/projects/mulehunter

- **Indian Digital Payment Intelligence Corporation (IDPIC)** — incorporated 16 October 2025 as a Section 8 (not-for-profit) company under the Companies Act 2013.<cite index="17-1">Indian Digital Payment Intelligence Corporation (IDPIC) has been incorporated as section 8 company, under the Companies Act, 2013 on 16/10/2025. Its primary mandate is to detect, prevent, and analyse fraud in India's rapidly expanding digital payments ecosystem in real time by leveraging cutting-edge technologies, including Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML), and Big Data Analytics.</cite>
 - Future roadmap: intended as the shared national fraud-intelligence layer for all digital payment rails.
 - Limitation: too new (< 9 months old) to have published performance data.
 - Official link: PIB press release — https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2244478

- **Master Direction on Counterfeit Notes, 2025** — mandates machine-based note authentication at every teller counter and back office, bans returning or destroying detected FICN at branch level, and imposes penalties for non-compliance.<cite index="44-1">Banknotes tendered over the counter shall be examined for authenticity through machines... In no case, the Counterfeit Notes shall be returned to the tenderer or destroyed by the bank branches. Failure of the banks to impound Counterfeit Notes detected at their end will be construed as wilful involvement.</cite>
 - Limitation: mandates *detection process*, not detection *technology* — hardware standard (UV/IR/magnetic) is decades old and does not require AI/computer vision.
 - Official link: RBI Master Directions portal — https://www.rbi.org.in

- **RBI Annual Report 2025-26 FICN Disclosures** — total FICN detected rose 5.7% to 229,746 pieces; Rs 500 notes rose 20.5% to 141,907 pieces; **97.6% detected by commercial banks vs 2.4% by RBI itself** (down from 7.9% in 2023-24), reflecting reduced RBI-side note indent/processing rather than improved detection technology.<cite index="41-1">Detection at the Reserve Bank itself accounted for just 2.4% of total FICNs in 2025-26, down from 7.9% in 2023-24 and 4.7% in 2024-25 — with the remaining 97.6% caught by other banks.</cite><cite index="42-1">the number of fake Rs 500 notes detected in the banking system increased by 20.50 per cent to 141,907 pieces during the year, as against 117,722 pieces detected in 2024-25.</cite>
 - Official link: RBI Annual Report — https://www.rbi.org.in

- **RBI FREE-AI Framework** (Framework for Responsible and Ethical Enablement of AI) — governance principles for AI adoption by regulated entities, covering algorithmic bias, model opacity, systemic risk.<cite index="19-1">the RBI has articulated a broader governance regime for AI through its Framework for Responsible and Ethical Enablement of Artificial Intelligence (FREE-AI).</cite>
 - Limitation: principles-based, not a binding technical standard.

- **Draft Third Amendment Directions, 2026** (effective 1 July 2026) — new customer-liability and compensation framework for digital banking fraud, including an 85%/₹25,000 (whichever lower) small-value compensation mechanism, and codified definitions of bank vs customer vs third-party negligence.<cite index="4-1">If an individual customer suffers a genuine fraudulent transaction loss of up to ₹50,000, they may be eligible to receive 85% of the net loss or up to ₹25,000, whichever is lower, once in their lifetime.</cite>
 - Excludes small finance banks, payments banks, RRBs, local area banks — a coverage gap for the most financially vulnerable segments.
 - Official link: RBI press release — https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/BS_PressReleaseDisplay.aspx?prid=62340

- **Central Payment Fraud Information Registry (CPFIR)** — AI/ML-based tracking and reporting system for payment fraud across the banking system.<cite index="36-1">The RBI has implemented a Central Payment Fraud Information Registry (CPFIR), a system to track and report payment frauds using AI and machine learning models.</cite>

## 1.4 Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre (I4C)

- **e-Zero FIR** (May 2025) — automatic conversion of NCRP/1930-reported complaints into First Information Reports.<cite index="14-1">The I4C portal also introduced the e-Zero FIR initiative in May 2025. - **62 banks onboarded to the I4C portal** as of 31 Dec 2025, with a target of onboarding *all* banking/financial institutions, including cooperatives, by December 2026.<cite index="14-1">Union Home and Cooperation Minister Amit Shah, on February 10, revealed that 62 banks had onboarded onto the Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre (I4C) by December 31, 2025... the Central Government was planning to onboard all banking and financial institutions, including cooperative banks, onto the I4C portal before December 2026.</cite>
- Scale: NCRP accessed 230M+ times (Jan 2020–Dec 2025); 8.2M+ complaints registered; only 184,000 converted into FIRs.<cite index="14-1">Shah also disclosed that the I4C reporting portal had been accessed over 230 million times from January 2020 and December 2025... over 8.2 million cybercrime-related complaints had been registered on the I4C portal as of November 30, 2025. - **Pratibimb** — geospatial crime-mapping platform pinpointing physical locations of mobile numbers linked to cybercrime, jointly run with JCCT Jamtara.<cite index="13-1">The Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre (I4C) and Joint Cybercrime Coordination Teams (JCCT) Jamtara, Jharkhand has launched the 'Pratibimb' platform which not only aggregates data but also employs geospatial mapping to pinpoint the physical locations of mobile numbers associated with cybercrimes.</cite>

## 1.12 Digital India / Smart Cities Mission

- Smart Cities Mission's integrated command-and-control centres (ICCCs) are the physical infrastructure layer underpinning city-level facial recognition/CCTV surveillance (see Hyderabad example above), but **no unified national AI public-safety standard** connects Smart Cities ICCCs to I4C's Pratibimb or NCRB's crime database — each city's system has evolved independently, creating interoperability and data-sharing gaps that were flagged as a broader governance concern in this research (SPRF).
- Digital India's DPI (Digital Public Infrastructure) stack (Aadhaar, UPI, DigiLocker) is simultaneously the platform enabling India's payments scale *and*, per multiple sources reviewed, the attack surface fraud actors now target, because "Zero Trust" architecture has not kept pace with DPI's growth.<cite index="62-1">Expansion of the Digital Attack Surface: The massive success of India's Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) has outpaced the implementation of robust "Zero Trust" security architectures, making the financial sector highly lucrative for scammers.</cite>

## 2.5 Counterfeit currency detection — commercial and research landscape

**This is the weakest commercial layer in the entire ecosystem.** Findings:

- **Legacy hardware dominates**: UV/IR/magnetic-ink currency-counting-cum-detection machines (e.g., AGS Transact's ProCash 6300) remain the standard commercial product sold to banks, retailers, and businesses across India.<cite index="75-1">Intelligent Money Counter-Cum-Detector RBI tried and tested Accurate detection of super fake notes in Rs.1000, 500, 100 denomination UV, IR, I-Scan Detection with LED indicator... - **AI/computer-vision counterfeit detection exists only as academic/hobbyist work**: IIT Kharagpur students built a smartphone app using 25 image-processing features (2019, not maintained/commercialized).<cite index="70-1">A user can upload a currency note image and the mobile app would verify its authenticity using 25 features extracted from the front and rear side of the currency note.</cite> Multiple GitHub/academic OpenCV and TensorFlow/Keras prototypes exist (e.g., aprameya2001/Fake-Currency-Detection-System) but are limited to ₹500/₹2000 denominations, trained on small custom datasets, and have no production deployment, support, or accuracy validation against current note series.<cite index="76-1">This currency detection currently works only for Indian Currency notes of denomination 500 and 2000. - **No commercially available, India-market mobile AI counterfeit-detection product was identified in this research** — a material market gap given that 97.6% of FICN detection now happens outside the RBI, largely at bank counters using legacy hardware, and consumer-facing citizens have essentially no tool at all.

# PART 3 — Competitor Analysis

## 3.1 What the current landscape actually covers

| Capability needed (per RFP brief) | Best current coverage | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| Digital arrest scam detection/alerting (call-flow, script, video metadata) | Partial — CyMAC/ASTR/Pratibimb address *identity* and *location* signals, not real-time in-call scam detection | Government only; no commercial product identified |
| Counterfeit currency computer-vision agent | Weak — legacy hardware + unmaintained academic prototypes | No serious commercial player |

| Citizen-facing multi-channel fraud shield | Partial — Chakshu/1930/cybercrime.gov.in handle reporting; no conversational, predictive, pre-transaction verdict tool for citizens exists at scale | Fragmented, reactive |

## 3.2 What none of them solve

1. **No product detects an active digital-arrest scam call in real time and intervenes before money moves.** All current tooling (MuleHunter.AI, CPFIR, NPCI risk scoring) intervenes at the *transaction* layer, after the victim has already decided to pay — the psychological-coercion, in-call layer is entirely uninstrumented.
2. **No consumer-facing AI counterfeit-currency detector exists.** The entire detection burden sits with banks using 20-year-old hardware; individual citizens and small merchants (who handle the majority of cash transactions) have zero tooling.
3. **No unified, cross-agency, legally admissible intelligence package format.** I4C (Pratibimb), RBI (MuleHunter.AI/CPFIR), NPCI (risk scoring), and state police operate on separate data models; the Supreme Court's own intervention was necessary precisely because no existing system linked telecom, banking, and judicial data automatically.
4. **No system explains its confidence/reasoning to a bank teller or call-centre agent in real time** — most tools listed here are backend risk-scoring APIs, not human-in-the-loop decision-support interfaces for frontline staff.
5. **Global fraud platforms (Sardine, Unit21, Bureau ID) are not tuned to India's specific attack patterns** — UPI-specific QR-code cloning, digital-arrest video-call scripts, and rupee-denominated mule layering are not native detection categories in Western platforms built around ACH/wire/card rails.
6. **NCRB's data gap on AI-driven cybercrime** means there is no authoritative, government-verified baseline against which any new AI safety product's efficacy could be measured nationally.

## 3.3 Missing features (specific, buildable)

- Real-time call/video-metadata classifier trained specifically on digital-arrest script patterns (fake police backgrounds, uniform detection, forged-document visual artifacts).
- On-device, phone-camera-based counterfeit note verification usable by any citizen or small merchant — no such product exists today at any maturity level.
- A cross-agency, evidentiary-standard data-exchange format that could serve I4C, RBI, CBI, and state police simultaneously (a genuine "digital chain of custody" for fraud intelligence).
- A multilingual (12+ regional language), IVR/WhatsApp-accessible "verdict" tool for elderly/rural citizens — most existing tools assume smartphone literacy and English/Hindi fluency.
- Explainable AI outputs formatted for direct submission as court evidence — none of the reviewed products claim court-admissibility as a design goal.

## 3.4 Opportunities

- **Counterfeit detection is the single largest whitespace**: build a lightweight, denomination-complete (₹10 through ₹500, post-₹2000-withdrawal) computer-vision model deployable on ordinary smartphone cameras, targeting bank tellers first (highest volume, existing mandate under RBI Master Direction 2025) then citizens/merchants.
- **Pre-transaction, in-call scam interception**: a real-time classifier integrated with telecom carriers (leveraging existing DoT Digital Intelligence Unit infrastructure) that can flag "digital arrest" call patterns *during* the call, not after money moves.
- **Cross-agency graph intelligence-as-a-service**: position between RBI's MuleHunter.AI (bank-side) and I4C's Pratibimb (telecom/location-side) to fuse both into a single victim-protection and prosecution-ready intelligence layer — directly answering the Supreme Court's own question to the RBI about why AI/ML tools aren't more widely used.
- **Multilingual conversational citizen shield**: build on top of existing 1930/Chakshu infrastructure rather than replacing it, adding a predictive "before you pay" layer.

## 4.1 RBI reports and statistics

- **RBI Annual Report FY2024-25** (released 28 May 2025): only 4.7% of FICN detected in banking system was RBI-side; 95.3% by commercial banks; decline in ₹10/20/50/100/2,000 counterfeit detections.<cite index="40-1">According to the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) Annual Report for 2024–25, released on May 28, 2025, only 4.7 per cent of the total FICNs detected in the banking system were identified by the RBI, while the remaining 95.3 per cent were discovered by commercial banks.</cite>
- **RBI Annual Report FY2025-26**: total FICN detections up 5.7% to 229,746 pieces; ₹500 fakes up 20.5% to 141,907 pieces (vs 117,722 in FY25); ₹20 fakes up 47.4% (253→373 pieces); ₹2,000 fakes falling (3,508→824) as that denomination is withdrawn; RBI-side detection down to 2.4% of total.<cite index="42-1">The number of counterfeit Rs 20 notes detected by the banking system increased to 373 pieces in 2025-26 from 253 pieces a year earlier, marking a 47.40 per cent rise.</cite><cite index="42-1">Detection of counterfeit Rs 2,000 notes fell to 824 pieces in 2025-26 from 3,508 pieces in the previous year.</cite>
- Security-printing expenditure fell from ₹6,372.8 crore to ₹4,875.2 crore, attributed to reduced note indent rather than efficiency gains.<cite index="41-1">expenditure on security printing during 2025-26 was ₹4,875.2 crore, down from ₹6,372.8 crore the previous year, reflecting the lower indent.</cite>

- NIA extradition case: a key accused in a 2013 Kasaragod (Kerala) FICN trafficking case (₹31 lakh worth trafficked from UAE) was extradited from the UAE and arrested at Mumbai airport in June 2025.<cite index="40-1">on June 20, 2025, the NIA arrested Moideenabha Ummer Beary alias Moideen at Mumbai airport in Maharashtra, a key accused in the 2013 Kasaragod FICN cases, following his extradition from the United Arab Emirates.</cite>

## 4.3 Detection methods (current state)

- **Regulatory-mandated**: machine-based examination at every counter/back-office/currency-chest; stamping and impounding protocol; branch-level penalty for non-compliance.<cite index="45-1">Banknotes tendered over the counter shall be examined for authenticity through machines. Similarly, banknotes received directly at the back office / currency chest through bulk tenders shall also be examined through machines.</cite>
- **Hardware**: UV/IR/magnetic-ink/I-Scan detection integrated into note-counting machines is the de facto national standard.<cite index="75-1">UV, IR, I-Scan Detection with LED indicator Preferred by India's top banks, corporate, small medium business owners.</cite>
- **Computer-vision approaches (research stage)**: OpenCV feature-extraction (watermark, security-thread, serial-number pattern) and deep-learning (TensorFlow/Keras CNN classifiers) approaches exist in academic literature and student projects, achieving good accuracy on small custom datasets but not validated at production scale or across the full current note series.<cite index="74-1">This system mainly focuses on the image classification aspect of deep learning primarily used to recognize currencies using Tensorflow and Keras library.</cite>

## 4.4 Public datasets and research projects for counterfeit detection

See consolidated dataset table in Part 5 below (banknote-authentication family). Notably, **all major public datasets are UCI/Kaggle's "Banknote Authentication" dataset family**, which is a **Euro-banknote wavelet-feature dataset**, not Indian Rupee-specific — meaning any AI counterfeit-detection build for India would need a **custom-curated Indian Rupee image dataset**, which currently does not exist in a large, public, standardized form. This is a second major whitespace: **no public, large-scale, labeled Indian Rupee counterfeit-image dataset was found**, forcing any serious effort to invest in primary data collection (in partnership with RBI/banks) before model development can begin.

## Closing Note on Evidence and Scope

This report draws on RBI, MHA, CERT-In, I4C, NPCI, TRAI, Supreme Court, Lok Sabha, and NCRB primary sources wherever official documents were available and citable, supplemented by verifiable industry reporting (MediaNama, Business Standard, LiveLaw, Tracxn) for facts not published in primary government form (e.g., bank-onboarding counts, funding rounds). Two structural evidence gaps are worth flagging directly: **(1)** the RBI itself will not disclose MuleHunter.AI outcome data, so its real-world efficacy cannot be independently verified from public sources; and **(2)** NCRB does not track AI-driven cybercrime as a distinct category, so no authoritative national baseline exists for measuring whether any new AI safety intervention — public or private — actually reduces losses over time. Any go-to-market or hackathon-judging strategy built on this report should treat closing those two evidence gaps as a first-order deliverable, not an afterthought.
